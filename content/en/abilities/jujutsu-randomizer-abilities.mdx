The safest way to build pressure is to pair a setup skill with a finisher. That is why stun-and-burst routes win so many fights. The game rewards players who convert small openings into full sequences instead of fishing for one giant swing every time.

<Section icon={Target} title="How to Read a Roll in 30 Seconds">

A strong player does not memorize every skill in the game. Instead, they identify the roll quickly and play the round around that roll. The goal is simple: learn your kit before you start swinging.

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Find your opener">
    Look for the first skill that creates momentum. That may be a stun, a dash, a teleport, or a pull.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="Find your closer">
    Identify the move that actually converts the opening into damage. This is often a burst, slash, or punch string.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="Find your escape">
    Check whether you have a dash, swap, heal, or invulnerability tool. If you do, you can play more aggressively.
  </Step>
  <Step num="4" title="Decide your fight range">
    Some rolls want close range, others want mid range, and a few only shine inside a domain or crowded cluster.
  </Step>
</Steps>

| Roll Pattern | What It Usually Means | Best Decision |
|---|---|---|
| Control + burst | Strong duel kit | Force one-on-one trades |
| Mobility + damage | Chase kit | Pressure low HP targets |
| Defense + burst | Counter kit | Bait attacks first |
| Domain + control | Teamfight kit | Wait for grouped enemies |
| Self-cost + damage | Risk kit | Only use when the trade is worth it |

<Callout type="warning" title="Do Not Tunnel">
A flashy skill does not always mean an easy win. If your opener misses, reset the fight instead of donating cooldowns.
</Callout>

<Section icon={Shield} title="Risk Skills, Domains, and Counterplay">

Risk skills are the most exciting part of Jujutsu Randomizer abilities, but they punish impatience. Some tools consume HP, some depend on a low-health condition, and some become much stronger only when the arena is crowded. That means the correct play is usually restraint, not panic.

Domains follow the same rule. They look strongest when they catch multiple players in a tight space. If the target can simply leave, you lose much of the value. Use domain pressure to control the map, not to chase one opponent across the arena.

<Section icon={Sparkles} title="Ability Cheat Sheet for Better Match Wins">

The best ability decisions are usually boring. You win more rounds by choosing the right fight shape than by trying to force the prettiest combo. If your roll has a control tool, lead with it. If it has a dash, use it to secure spacing. If it has a self-cost move, wait until the payoff is obvious.

This simple priority system keeps you from wasting strong skills in the wrong situation. It also helps you survive when the lobby is full of unpredictable players and overlapping pressure.

| Situation | Best Ability Type | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| Enemy is rushing | Control or knockback | Stops the approach and creates space |
| Enemy is low HP | Fast burst or teleport | Ends the fight before they reset |
| You are surrounded | AoE or domain | Hits multiple targets at once |
| You need to escape | Dash, swap, or heal | Buys time and resets tempo |
| You have a stun | Heavy burst finisher | Converts setup into reliable damage |

<Callout type="success" title="Best Practice">
Use one setup skill, one damage skill, and one escape plan. That structure fits most random rolls.
</Callout>
